# Sylvia Training Inputs
### How to Accelerate Development Beyond Fixed Sweeps

> The problem is not "how do I make Sylvia choose?"
> It's "how do I create enough structured decision substrate for Sylvia to become better at choosing on her own?"

---

## The Core Insight

3–4 explicit sweeps a day is too slow if the goal is emergence of a real ratio lattice.

That cadence can **maintain continuity**, but it cannot rapidly **deepen judgment** unless the system is also harvesting signal between those sweeps.

Sylvia needs more input, but not just more volume. She needs more **decision-relevant, structured, metabolizable signal**.

---

## The Real Training Fuel

Sylvia develops fastest when she can observe:
- Contrasts
- Rankings
- Consequences
- Reversals
- Preferences
- Tensions
- Unfinishedness
- Evidence of what actually mattered

Not "more tasks." **More comparative traces.**

---

## The Four Training Streams

### Stream 1: Passive Capture
Signal that flows in without active work:
- Calendar events
- Slack messages
- ClickUp tasks
- Reopen patterns
- Document churn
- Event packets
- Incoming obligations

### Stream 2: Active Micro-Judgment
Explicit comparative acts:
- Pairwise comparisons
- Ranking sets
- "Why this, not that?"
- Forced next-move selection

### Stream 3: Calibration
Learning from outcomes:
- Later verdicts
- Regret logs
- Downstream consequence receipts
- False positive / false negative analysis

### Stream 4: Architecture Reflection
Meta-level awareness:
- Where choosing felt weak
- Where dimensions seemed missing
- Where comparators conflicted
- Where the system misread importance

## High-Value Training Input Types

### 1. Choice Logs (Highest Value)
Any time you choose A over B, that is lattice fuel.

**Format:**
```
candidate_a: [option]
candidate_b: [option]
winner: [which one]
reason: [one sentence]
later_verdict: [optional correction]
```

**Examples:**
- "I worked on architecture instead of outreach."
- "I postponed revenue work to close a continuity gap."
- "I answered this person first because relational consequence was higher."
- "I ignored this urgent-looking thing because it was counterfeit momentum."

Even 5–10 of these per day would train Sylvia faster than one big abstract reflection.

### 2. Friction Logs
Anything you avoid, delay, circle, reopen, or keep half-touching is highly informative.

Friction often reveals:
- Counterfeit risk
- Cognitive cost
- Identity threat
- Relational charge
- Structural ambiguity

**Log things like:**
- "I keep not doing this."
- "This keeps coming back."
- "This becomes muddy every time I touch it."
- "I don't know why this repels me."

These are not failures. These are deep training signals.

### 3. Post-Decision Reflections
Very short. Not essays.

For any meaningful move:
- Was it right?
- What did I underweight?
- What did I overweight?
- What hidden variable showed up?

That is how Sylvia starts calibrating.

### 4. Regret and Reversal Logs
One of the fastest ways to train the lattice:
- What you chose
- What happened
- Whether it was right
- What you now think should have been weighted more heavily

**Examples:**
- "I over-weighted closure and under-weighted leverage."
- "I treated clarity work as procrastination, but it was actually prerequisite work."
- "I thought this was urgent, but it was just emotionally loud."

That is how dimensions sharpen.

### 5. Voice Memo Ingress
Since you think while moving, driving, pacing, and noticing patterns, voice memos could become raw substrate.

Not polished. Just:
- "Here's what I'm noticing"
- "Here's what I avoided"
- "Here's what feels structurally off"
- "Here's the thing that's louder than it deserves to be"

This would multiply Sylvia's training material.

### 6. Comparative Prompts (Not Open Prompts)
Do not ask Sylvia broad questions like:
- "What matters?"
- "What should I do?"

Ask pairwise or ranked questions:
- Which matters more, and why?
- Which is more dangerous to ignore?
- Which creates more leverage?
- Which is more identity-aligned?
- Which is fake urgency?

Comparative data is much richer than descriptive data.

### 7. Daily "Surprise Me" Extraction
Have Sylvia scan the day and extract:
- The most underestimated thing
- The most overestimated thing
- The quietest but most important thing
- The thing that kept generating secondary consequences
- The thing avoided for the wrong reason

That trains discovery, not just obedience.

### 8. Pairwise Comparisons at Scale
Instead of defining dimensions abstractly, generate hundreds of micro-comparisons:

```
X vs Y
- which wins under action_now?
- which wins under long_leverage?
- which wins under identity_alignment?
- which wins under reduce_ambiguity?
```

Over time, the system can infer much more stable dimension behavior.

### 9. Repeated Ranking from Different Lenses
Take the same set of objects and rank them under different comparators.

That teaches Sylvia:
- Importance is not one-dimensional
- Different questions produce different orderings
- The meaning of "best" is conditional

### 10. Consequence Journals
For meaningful moves, later note:
- What happened because of that choice
- What got easier
- What got worse
- What new work it generated
- Whether it actually mattered

This converts judgment into calibration.

## What to Build (Priority Order)

### 1. Micro Choice Capture System
Something dead simple:
- Two options
- Chosen option
- One-sentence reason
- Optional later correction

Frictionless enough to do all day.

### 2. Daily Comparator Drill
Every day, have Sylvia compare 5–20 objects under 1–3 comparators.
Not essays. Just rankings and brief reasons.

### 3. Regret / Reversal Ledger
A place where you explicitly log:
- Wrong calls
- False urgencies
- Missed leverage
- Underestimated relational consequence
- Ambiguity that should have been clarified earlier

### 4. Reopen-Loop Detector
Track what keeps coming back:
- Docs reopened repeatedly
- Tasks bounced forward multiple times
- People you keep meaning to answer
- Architecture topics that recur but never resolve

Recurring return is one of the strongest signals of latent importance.

### 5. Voice Memo Ingress Path
A way to capture spontaneous comparative thoughts while driving, walking, thinking out loud.

### 6. "Why This Over That?" Habit
Even once or twice an hour, capture:
- What you just did
- What you didn't do instead
- Why the winner won

Almost direct ratio-lattice training.

## The Deeper Truth

You are not trying to "feed Sylvia more stuff."

You are trying to let Sylvia observe:
- How a mind encounters the world
- How it sorts signal from noise
- How it chooses under ambiguity
- How it learns from consequence

That is the real training stream.

You do not need ten times more manual work. You need **better forms of capture** so your existing cognition leaves behind more usable traces.

## What Sylvia Needs From Jake Most

Not more commands.
Not even more documentation, mainly.

What she needs most is **your live comparative cognition made capturable.**

Meaning:
- How you distinguish real from fake importance
- How you detect structural rot
- How you smell drift
- How you notice hidden leverage
- How you recognize when clarity is the real work
- How you separate dread from truth
- How you decide when to push, wait, clarify, escalate, or route

That is the actual training set.

---

## Key Principle

You do not need Sylvia to fully understand everything in order to develop.

You need Sylvia to accumulate enough repeated comparative examples that stable judgment patterns begin to emerge.

The question becomes:
Not "How do I teach her the whole architecture?"
But: **"How do I create thousands of small judgment traces?"**

That is much more doable.
